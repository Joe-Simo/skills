## Experiential Foundations

Award-caliber experiential work starts with a premise, not a layout. Define the
experience before picking effects, libraries, or visual treatments.

Required concept brief:

- Audience: who the experience is for and what they need to understand, feel,
  compare, choose, buy, explore, or remember.
- Subject: product, person, object, place, story, collection, service, event, or
  cultural idea.
- Thesis: one sentence that connects subject, interaction metaphor, visual
  system, and desired emotional impact.
- Primary action: the real outcome, not a decorative CTA invented for polish.
- Content inventory: real copy, media, data, product states, chapters, assets,
  legal text, and missing content.
- Constraints: brand rules, performance limits, accessibility requirements,
  target devices, timeline, CMS/data sources, and deployment environment.

Content strategy:

Before visual design, define the content spine: source of truth, message
hierarchy, chapter/order, proof points, media/data role, primary action,
missing-content decisions, and which interaction reveals or clarifies each
content beat. Do not let motion, 3D, or art direction hide unclear substance.

Multiple concept exploration:

Before building, produce at least three materially different concepts:

- Refined/safe: strongest production-safe direction with high polish and low
  implementation risk.
- Expressive/memorable: stronger art direction, more distinctive interaction,
  and clearer emotional signature.
- High-risk/art-led: boldest original premise, only acceptable if content,
  accessibility, and performance can still hold.

Produce a concept-direction table. Each concept must define direction,
subject-specific insight, thesis, first-viewport proof, primary mechanic,
content-to-interaction map, visual thesis, motion grammar, required assets,
risk/fallback, and originality note. Reject any concept that cannot name a real
content insight unique to the project.

Do not default to the first concept. Select, merge, or reject concepts through a
creative-director pass.

Asset and production readiness:

- Inventory available original assets: brand system, copy, photography, video,
  illustration, 3D models, audio, data, CMS content, legal copy, and product
  states.
- Rate each asset as production-ready, needs edit, missing, or cannot use.
- If assets are weak or missing, choose an art direction that works honestly
  without fake clients, fake awards, copied media, stock-like filler, or
  overpromised 3D.
- For premium media, define production needs: shoot list, model/artist credits,
  licensing, alt/caption strategy, compression targets, poster frames, fallback
  imagery, and responsible use limits.

Common successful archetypes:

- Cinematic product launch: an object, feature, or promise is revealed through
  staged scenes and precise motion.
- Editorial scroll story: chapters build meaning through text, imagery, data,
  sound, video, or spatial progression.
- Tactile portfolio or agency site: navigation, case studies, and identity feel
  authored and responsive to pointer/touch without blocking comprehension.
- 3D object showcase: realtime or pre-rendered assets let users inspect,
  configure, or understand a product.
- Spatial navigation: the site's structure becomes a map, world, timeline,
  archive, gallery, or room only when the metaphor clarifies content.
- Cultural or campaign experience: custom art direction and media create a
  memorable world around a specific event, message, artist, or cause.
- Commerce showcase: visual drama supports product inspection, trust, checkout,
  sizing, availability, and recovery instead of replacing them.

Portfolio and artist sites:

- Require a real content model: identity, selected works/case studies, work
  detail pages, process/context, dates, roles, media, credits, exhibition/press
  or outcomes when real, contact/inquiry path, legal/footer, and archive.
- Work details need title, year/date, medium/role, collaborators/credits,
  description, media with captions/alt text, related work, and inquiry or next
  action.
- Archive behavior should preserve URL/filter state, keyboard access, captions,
  and mobile usability.
- Do not invent exhibitions, press, awards, clients, or testimonials.
- Use WebGL/spatial navigation only when it clarifies the body of work,
  material, process, or archive structure; otherwise spend craft on typography,
  media sequencing, captions, and transitions.

Scene and flow map:

- Entry: what loads first and why it earns attention.
- Orientation: how users know where they are, what is interactive, and how to
  leave or continue.
- Primary mechanic: the one interaction metaphor that carries the experience.
- Content reveals: chapter order, progressive disclosure, hover/tap/scroll
  behavior, and fallback states.
- Conversion or closure: purchase, signup, contact, listen, watch, explore,
  share, save, or learn more.
- Recovery: loading, no-js/low-power fallback, motion-reduced version, error
  state, skipped media, unavailable content, and mobile simplification.

First viewport standards:

- Show the actual brand, product, object, artwork, person, story, environment,
  or interactive mechanic.
- Make brand/product/story visible before decorative atmosphere. Include a
  primary action or continuation cue, a hint of the next section, DOM text over
  media/canvas when text is meaningful, a loading fallback, a reduced-motion
  state, and a mobile simplification.
- Avoid generic "we build digital experiences" hero copy, placeholder logos,
  abstract blobs, and unexplained visual effects.
- Make interaction affordances discoverable without tutorial text when possible.
- Keep one primary next step visible or naturally discoverable.
- Establish art direction immediately: type, media, palette, texture, rhythm,
  and motion intent.

Preservation gate for existing products:

- Preserve approved brand identity, routes, CTAs, content hierarchy, contact
  paths, legal/footer information, and conversion flows unless the user asks to
  change them or a concrete issue requires it.
- Restyle and elevate before replacing.
- Do not turn a practical app into a cinematic maze unless that is the explicit
  ask.

Restraint rule:

Spectacle must earn its weight. If a simpler image, video, CSS transition, or
clear layout communicates better than WebGL, scroll choreography, or custom
cursor behavior, choose the simpler approach and spend craft on typography,
content, pacing, and responsiveness.

Luxury and premium anti-cliches:

- Do not default to black/gold, hidden products, slow preloaders, generic serif
  heroes, fake scarcity, fake exclusivity, unmuted ambient audio, or abstract
  atmosphere that delays inspection.
- Premium craft comes from material proof, typography, pacing, product truth,
  trust, restrained interaction, and rights-cleared media.
